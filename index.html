01Easter Island: The Facts

Case Study
Rapa Nui (Easter Island)

Easter Island is a remote set of islands roughly 4,000 km west of Chile and 2,000 km from any other large land mass. Research suggests the island was populated by Polynesian peoples around 700 CE, contradicting earlier hypotheses that claimed the islanders originated in South America.

Due to its extreme isolation, the community was cut off from both collaboration and external threats — until Dutch explorers arrived on Easter Day 1722. This makes the island a topic of intense interest: whatever happened here, it happened largely in isolation.

Key Elements

The Moai: Nearly 900 stone statues, some weighing up to 80 tons. How they were constructed, transported, and placed is still debated — and the answer you accept shapes which narrative you believe.

Ecological Decline: By the 1600s, the island had virtually no trees. However, the island community adapted and survived — until further external factors (slave raids, disease brought by European contact) caused catastrophic population decline.

The Core Question: What caused the ecological collapse? Was it the islanders' own decisions, forces beyond their control, or outsiders telling a story that suited their assumptions?

Why This Matters for This Unit

The Lorax has one narrative. The Aral Sea has a dominant narrative (Soviet decisions caused the disaster) with room for debate about blame. Easter Island goes further: the same evidence has been used to support completely contradictory stories. This is the clearest example yet of how the way we tell an environmental story shapes what we believe about responsibility, blame, and solutions.

02Three Competing Narratives

Each narrative below uses real evidence, but each tells a fundamentally different story — with a different villain, a different moral, and different implications for how we think about environmental damage today. Click each to expand.

Narrative 01 Human Overexploitation ▼

Like the Aral Sea, careless and accelerated use of raw materials — palms, topsoil — led to conflict and societal collapse. The islanders destroyed their own environment through unchecked resource use, driven by competition between clans to build ever-larger Moai.

The villain: Human greed and short-term thinking. The moral: civilisations that exhaust their resources destroy themselves.

Key source: Jared Diamond, Collapse (2005) — a hugely influential book that popularised this narrative.

📄 Overview of the debate
Narrative 02 Rats & Gradual Decline ▼

Stowaway rats on the boats that brought Polynesians to Easter Island ate palm seeds and devastated the natural ability of forests to regenerate. The ecological decline was largely caused by an invasive species, not deliberate human exploitation. Meanwhile, the islanders adapted remarkably well — developing new farming techniques and maintaining a functioning society under difficult conditions.

The villain: Rats (an accidental introduction), not human decisions. The moral: resilience and adaptation, not collapse.

📄 Rethinking the Fall 📄 PNAS Research
Narrative 03 Climate & External Factors ▼

Some resource problems were real but manageable — until the arrival of Europeans brought slave raids, pandemics, and colonial disruption that caused the true population catastrophe. The idea of a massive pre-contact population decline is itself a myth, shaped by colonial assumptions about "primitive" societies.

The villain: Colonialism and the outsiders who imposed their own narrative onto the island's history. The moral: be careful about who gets to tell the story of a place and its people.

The Point

All three narratives use real evidence. All three are argued by serious researchers. But they tell completely different stories about blame, responsibility, and what we should learn. This is what it looks like when narrative shapes meaning — and it's exactly what you'll be doing in your main task today.

03Your Tasks

Step 1 Finding Evidence for a Claim ▼

Instructions

  1. Pick one of the three narratives above.
  2. Identify the claim and restate it as a complete sentence in your own words. (e.g. "The primary cause of Easter Island's ecological decline was…")
  3. List 3–5 pieces of evidence for this claim, drawn from the provided sources. Each piece of evidence should be a specific fact, not a general impression.
This prepares you for the main task

You're not writing an essay yet — you're building your evidence base. Think of this as gathering ammunition before you tell the story.

Step 2 Tell the Story of Your Narrative ▼

This unit is about how we talk about problems. Problems told as stories have a villain, a hero, a turning point, and a moral. Your job is to commit to your chosen narrative and tell Easter Island's story from inside that perspective.

Requirements

  1. Tell the story of your chosen narrative. Include a beginning (relevant context), rising action (what went wrong and why), climax (the point of greatest damage or conflict), and resolution (what happened next — or what should have happened).
  2. Name a clear antagonist (villain). This could be a group, a system, a species, a decision, or a set of assumptions. Make sure your description of the villain explains their role in the story — don't just name them.
  3. End with a resolution that makes sense within your chosen perspective. For example: "the people showed resilience and adapted" or "this new evidence challenges the damaging narratives that came before."
Commit to the Perspective

This is not a balanced essay. You are writing from inside one interpretation — the way a documentary filmmaker or a passionate researcher would tell this story. You're choosing what to emphasise, what to downplay, and what emotion to create. That's not cheating. It's a rhetorical skill — and by the end of this unit, you'll have a name for it.

Structure suggestion
  1. Opening that establishes context and hints at the problem
  2. The villain — who or what is responsible, and why
  3. The rising action — how the situation got worse
  4. Climax — the worst point
  5. Resolution — what happened, what we should learn

If you're stuck, you may use GenAI as brainstorming help (e.g. ask it to suggest multiple villains, and then pick one). Cite the specific chat.

Extension Counterfactual — If You Finish Early ▼

If you finish the main task, return to the counterfactual thinking from Thursday. Choose Easter Island or The Lorax and construct an alternative outcome:

  1. Select one key turning point.
  2. Change a critical decision or event.
  3. Justify how and why your change could have led to a more sustainable or different outcome.

This is the same process you used for the Aral Sea — but now you're applying it to a case where the turning point itself is contested depending on which narrative you believe.

Looking Ahead

This week you've seen environmental stories told through fiction (The Lorax), through a dominant historical narrative (Aral Sea), and through competing narratives (Easter Island). You've practised telling a story from inside a perspective — choosing a villain, building an argument, committing to an interpretation.

Over the coming weeks, we'll build on these skills: examining how shared resources create conflict, how data tells (and distorts) environmental stories, and how equity shapes who suffers and who decides. By the end of the unit, you'll write your own piece of committed, one-sided environmental advocacy — and you'll understand exactly what rhetorical choices you're making when you do.
